Study Guide

Chapter 1 — Cancer Overview

  • Cell cycle: G0, G1, S, G2, M. G2 and M are most radiosensitive.
  • Benign tumors: well differentiated, noninvasive, do not metastasize, often encapsulated, slow-growing, pushing.
  • Malignant tumors: invasive, infiltrative, may metastasize, abnormal cells, may extend beyond pseudocapsule.
  • Anaplastic: undifferentiated cells that no longer look or function like the original tissue.
  • Sarcoma: mesenchymal/connective tissue origin. Carcinoma: epithelial tissue origin.
  • Screening: sensitivity = true positive; specificity = true negative.
  • TNM: T = tumor size/extent, N = lymph nodes, M = metastasis.
  • Biopsy: only way to know for certain if tissue is cancerous.
  • Neoadjuvant: before surgery. Adjuvant: with or after another treatment.
  • Brachytherapy: short-distance therapy using radioactive sources.

Chapter 2 — Ethics & Law

  • ASRT: professional society. ARRT: credentialing/certification body.
  • Seven principles: autonomy, beneficence, confidentiality, justice, nonmaleficence, role fidelity, veracity.
  • HIPAA: Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act.
  • Teleology: consequences. Deontology: rules/duty. Virtue ethics: character and practical wisdom.
  • Grief cycle: shock, denial, anger, bargaining, depression, testing, acceptance.
  • Assault: threat. Battery: touching without consent. False imprisonment: preventing a patient from leaving.
  • Libel: written defamation. Slander: spoken defamation.

Chapter 3 — Patient Assessment

  • Therapeutic relationship: healthcare worker and patient working together for beneficial change.
  • Cognitive content: facts and words. Affective content: feelings, attitudes, behaviors.
  • Reflective listening: responding with empathy.
  • Cachexia: muscle loss with or without fat loss.
  • Myelosuppression: reduced bone marrow function.
  • Anemia: low RBCs. Leukopenia: low WBCs. Thrombocytopenia: low platelets. Pancytopenia: all blood cells reduced.
  • Orthostatic hypotension: sudden BP drop when standing.
  • Transfer rule: transfer toward the patient’s stronger side.

Study Guide

1. Detection & Diagnosis Basics

  • Detection means finding possible disease early, often before symptoms appear.
  • Diagnosis means identifying the disease or condition.
  • Early detection improves survival, gives more treatment options, and can improve quality of life.
  • Prevention means strategies that stop cancer from developing.
  • Screening means selecting appropriate tests to check asymptomatic people for disease.
  • Hard-to-access cancers, like pancreas or ovary, are often found late because symptoms are nonspecific.

2. Signs, Symptoms, Syndromes

  • Sign: objective finding observed by examiner, such as rash or lump.
  • Symptom: subjective experience reported by patient, such as pain or fatigue.
  • Syndrome: group of related signs and symptoms from a common cause.
  • Paraneoplastic syndrome: symptoms caused by tumor-produced substances or hormones, occurring away from the tumor.

3. Medical Record & Patient Interview

  • The medical record documents past medical experiences, medical history, test results, progress notes, consent forms, correspondence, and medical images.
  • The medical history gives a snapshot of prior problems and treatments.
  • The patient interview is one of the physician’s most powerful tools.
  • Subjective data: patient complaints, feelings, and history.
  • Objective data: measurable or observable findings, such as vital signs, labs, imaging, or physical exam findings.
  • Technical jargon can interfere with the interview because the patient may not understand it.

4. Physical Examination

  • Inspection: looking.
  • Palpation: touching/feeling tissues and organs.
  • Percussion: tapping to evaluate underlying structures.
  • Auscultation: listening to body sounds, such as heart and lungs.
  • Edema: tissue swelling caused by excess fluid accumulation.

5. Vital Signs

  • Vital signs help determine whether a patient is stable for treatment.
  • They provide baseline data and help detect acute reactions or therapy-related changes.
  • Vital signs include temperature, pulse, respirations, blood pressure, pain, and oxygen saturation.
  • Homeostasis: body’s ability to maintain stable internal balance.

6. Temperature, Pulse, Respirations, Blood Pressure

  • Oral temperature: most common; normal approximately 97.7–99.5°F.
  • Rectal temperature: most accurate, often used for unstable patients.
  • Febrile: fever. Hypothermia: temperature below normal.
  • Radial pulse: most common pulse site. Carotid pulse: emergencies. Apical pulse: infants or cardiac patients.
  • Pulse factors: rate, rhythm, size, and tension.
  • Respiration factors: rate, depth, rhythm, and character.
  • Adult respirations: about 12–20 breaths/min.
  • Tachypnea: respiratory rate greater than 20/min. Bradypnea: decreased respiratory rate.
  • Dyspnea: difficult breathing. Orthopnea: shortness of breath when lying down. Apnea: absence of breathing.
  • Systolic: top number; heart squeezing. Diastolic: bottom number; heart resting.
  • Normal blood pressure is about 120/80 mmHg. Most common site is the brachial artery.

7. Pain & Oxygen

  • Pain is subjective and should be assessed with tools such as a numeric 0–10 scale or faces scale.
  • Acute pain: short duration, well-defined onset.
  • Chronic pain: persistent pain lasting more than 3 months.
  • Oxygen is classified as a drug and must be prescribed by a physician.
  • Use the lowest effective oxygen dose.
  • Excess oxygen can cause absorptive atelectasis or oxygen toxicity.
  • Nasal cannula is the most common device for low concentrations of oxygen.

8. Screening & Predictive Values

  • Prevalence: probability of disease in a population at a point in time.
  • Incidence: probability that disease develops in a disease-free patient during an interval.
  • Sensitivity: ability of a test to give a true-positive result when disease is present.
  • Specificity: ability of a test to give a true-negative result when disease is absent.
  • Mass screening depends on results obtained, cost-effectiveness, and risk to the patient.

9. Cancer Screening Highlights

  • Breast: mammography; average-risk women begin regular screening around age 45, with option to start 40–44.
  • Sentinel lymph node biopsy: identifies the first draining lymph node from a breast tumor using blue dye or technetium-99m.
  • Lung: leading cause of cancer-related deaths; low-dose CT helps detect high-risk patients earlier.
  • Prostate: screening uses DRE and PSA; discussion depends on risk and life expectancy.
  • Cervical: linked to HPV; screening uses Pap and/or HPV testing; vaccination does not replace screening.

10. Labs, Imaging, Biopsy, Staging

  • Blood studies look at blood cells; blood chemistry looks at chemicals like glucose, electrolytes, and proteins.
  • Microbiologic studies detect organisms that may cause infection.
  • Nuclear medicine uses radiopharmaceuticals; gamma rays are detected to create images.
  • PET uses radioactive glucose; cancer cells absorb glucose faster than normal tissue.
  • CT uses x-ray data to create cross-sectional images and is good for bone and anatomy.
  • MRI uses radiofrequency waves and a magnetic field; no ionizing radiation; excellent for soft tissue.
  • Ultrasound uses high-frequency sound waves; cannot detect microcalcifications.
  • Biopsy gives histologic confirmation and is essential for diagnosis.
  • Exfoliative cytology collects cells by scraping or natural sloughing.
  • Tumor markers are substances released by tumors and detected in blood or body fluids; PSA is one example.
  • TNM: T = primary tumor size/extent, N = regional lymph nodes, M = distant metastasis.
  • Histologic grade: how abnormal/aggressive cells look under the microscope.
